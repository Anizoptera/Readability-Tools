Vegard Farstad, Member of Planetary Society and Norwegian Astronautical Society since 1990

Yes.

600 years ago, Europeans didn’t know about Americans, Africans didn’t know about Australians, and Hawaiians didn’t know about almost anyone else!

600 years later, we have sent probes to every planet in our solar system (and several of the dwarf planets), landed humans on the moon, built several space stations, and have self-driving robots maneuvering on the surface of Mars.

600 years from now, humans will almost certainly be walking on other worlds outside our solar system.

Maybe it will take longer than that. But forever is a long time.

Jeff Mwangi, Stargazer
Answered May 3

The issue wouldn’t be if humans would ever walk on other planets outside our solar system. I believe those guys are capable of anything, really.

Our challenge is if we can sustain ourselves long enough to be able to do it. The average lifespan currently is just plain sad so…definitely not in our lifetime. We might be lucky enough to see boots landing on Mars but its still a long shot.

We currently have Voyager 1, a perfectly functioning, man-made spacecraft which just left the solar system. It took 40 years to get it there from launch, 1 billion dollars (250 million in 1977 adjusted for inflation) and 11,000 work years to prepare, equivalent to one-third of the effort used to build the Great Pyramids of Giza.

Furthermore, the nearest planet outside our solar system is in the Star system of Epsilon Eridani[1], only 10.5 light years away. Unfortunately, its a gas giant, meaning that it might not even have a ground to walk on. However, if we have means to transport humans that far, am sure we’ll have a way to walk on a measly gas giant. 1 Ly is about 9.5 trillion km. That would make our destination at just about 100 trillion km away. That would take the Voyager 1 at its current speed of 17,260 meters per second….**calculating**…a whopping 183,718 years!

That’s over 2,600 different sets of crews on the ship, assuming they leave earth and start service as babies. The point is, time is our number one challenge when it comes to space travel.

The numbers certainly don’t work in our favor. Even with some kind of mind blowing technologies, we won’t probably be able to make it outside our solar system anytime now. Fortunately, we are in that phase where our advances in technology is accelerating. Plus, the above scenario is using technology from 1977. If we develop better space flight and life sustaining technologies coupled with the hope that humans’ volatile nature will somehow not pop up and destroy us before that time, maybe we will be reporting from Epsilon Eridani.

So, yea, if humanity exists for long enough, anything is possible.

Eric Worrall, Director of Desirable Apps
Answered May 5

Yes. A starship capable of 5% of the speed of light was designed in the 1950s by the Manhattan scientists, the people who developed the first atom bomb, using known technology.

Project Orion (nuclear propulsion) - Wikipedia

Building the ship would not require any extraordinary advances in technology. The ship components are the same scale and materials as components of large military warships such as aircraft carriers, so could easily be fabricated by existing engineering shops.

The nearest likely Earthlike planet orbits Proxima Centauri, 4.2 lightyears from Earth. At 5% of the speed of light that is an 80 year journey - a long journey, but not unimaginable.

Obviously the ship would have to be multi-generational, the descendants of the original crew would be the people to walk on the alien world, though it is possible young members of the crew would survive the full journey.

“Ever” is such an incredibly long time. If we can accept that the probability of an event is >0, then given enough time, the chances of it happening increase.

The physical constraints of interstellar flight are considerable (to put it mildly), but surmountable with 21st-century engineering (i.e. a generation ship). I can easily imagine that in 10,000 years, the problems involved may have easier solutions.

But my question is, in 10,000 years, will “we” still be “humanity?”

David Haigh, I work in Tech. I paint.
Updated May 5

Lots of life-reaffirming, spiritual positive answers here.

Most compare finding the Americas and technological advances of history.

These were all evolutions however. Boats became incrementally more stable, navigation became a necessity and was improved and people wanted new trade routes and treasure. Opportunities become more available. Suddenly the Americas are found; primarily using a better boat.

Einstein based his work upon Newton, Maxwell and others. Evolution — it's how discovery/science works to-date.

Exo-planets we can walk on are a long, long, long way away.

There is an undeniable revolution needed to get to exo-planets. Basically we cannot get to them unless someone works out how to bend spacetime enough to make a spaceship appear in an exo-planet's orbit. This may not be possible at all.

No evolution of current technology can get us there.

I have no idea whether it is possible or how far this quantum leap is away and neither does anyone else.

If I were to guess; I'd consider the ingenuity of humanity, the fact that progress ebbs and wanes, the fact that humans have lived for a tenth of a percent as long as dinosaurs and the fact that 99% of all species are now extinct.

I'd say 50:50.

Answered May 3

No. It won’t happen this century, unless something very disruptive is discovered/invented. Probably won’t happen next neither. And to happen in a not so far future, our technological civilization must not fall while that.

So, we will keep advancing in other sciences (including genetics, and our own genes that we are already modifying), and we will want to maximize the odds of success of the mission. So probably whatever we send, won’t be human anymore.

But that is about individuals. The ability to send a sizable amount of our population to other solar systems (to properly talk about humanity) probably won’t be at our reach.

And while that, the clock is ticking, if we keep, like today, advancing in power but not in maturity the risk of somewhat ending ourselves increases with time, even if we don’t get caught by external factors.
